1. Abstract
Manufacturing companies operating under strict compliance and government contract
requirements face major challenges in maintaining traceability, efficiency, and data security.
Many rely on outdated barcode systems or manual tracking, leading to inefficiencies and human
error. To address these issues, AIBI Solutions, in collaboration with KMD Technology
Solutions, is developing an on-premises computer vision system that automates barcode scanning
and verifies manufacturing processes in real time. The system uses fixed cameras at each
workstation to automatically detect and decode multiple barcodes, monitor process steps, and
provide immediate feedback through an intuitive interface. Data is formatted into structured
JSON outputs and integrated securely with KMD’s existing workflow management system
through a local API connector, ensuring full compatibility without cloud dependencies.
The project aims to increase production efficiency, reduce human error, and enhance operational
transparency while maintaining data privacy and regulatory compliance. Beyond the immediate
business value, the system demonstrates how artificial intelligence and computer vision can
bring smart automation to secure, high stakes manufacturing environments, paving the way for
broader industrial modernization.
This report documents the results of the requirements engineering and design process. It includes
the project background and objectives, elicited user and system requirements, and
implementation of details of the proposed computer vision solution.

3. Problem Statement
3.1. Business Background
AIBI Solutions was founded as an AI-focused sister company of KMD Technology Solutions, a
company known for its project management software and workflow optimization tools. While
KMD focused on improving digital coordination and project efficiency, AIBI was created to
extend that mission into artificial intelligence, with a focus on secure, on-premises systems built
for regulated industries.
The goal behind AIBI Solutions is to deliver advanced AI technology that can operate entirely
within a company’s local infrastructure, allowing manufacturers that work under government
contracts or strict compliance regulations to take advantage of automation without compromising
security. The company’s first major product was a large language model–based quoting system
designed to speed up and standardize the part quoting process. It reduced manual effort,
increased consistency across quotes, and helped teams respond faster to customer requests while
keeping all sensitive data stored on-site.
AIBI’s next step is expanding into computer vision. This project focuses on building an
intelligent tracking system that continuously monitors parts as they move through the facility,
verifying that workers follow each step in the correct order. The goal is to bring greater
transparency, efficiency, and accountability to manufacturing workflows, while maintaining
strict compliance and ensuring that all processing happens locally, within the client’s own secure
environment.

3.2. Needs
Manufacturing companies that work under government contracts or strict compliance standards
are under constant pressure to keep their production accurate, traceable, and fully accountable.
Many still rely on manual tracking or outdated barcode systems that slow down operations and
leave room for mistakes. At the same time, stricter data protection rules make it difficult to adopt
cloud-based AI tools. These companies need solutions that can run completely on-premises,
systems that deliver the speed and intelligence of modern AI without sending any data off-site.
AIBI Solutions was built to meet that need. The goal is to bring automation and insight into
manufacturing environments that can’t rely on traditional cloud infrastructure, combining large
language models and computer vision to make production faster, safer, and more reliable.

3.3. Objectives
The objective of this phase is to develop a computer vision system that keeps track of parts and
verifies that workers are completing each step in the correct order. The system will automatically
monitor parts as they move through the facility, ensure each process follows the proper sequence,
and alert supervisors when something is out of order. Everything will run locally to stay
compliant with strict security requirements.

4.1.3.2. User Scenarios
UC-001: Scan Part Barcodes
▪

A factory operator places a paper containing barcodes within the camera's field of view.
The system automatically detects and decodes all visible barcodes, maps them to
configured data fields, and outputs structured JSON data to the downstream system. If
barcodes fail to decode, the system alerts the operator.

UC-002: Monitor Manufacturing Step
▪

A factory operator performs manufacturing steps while the system continuously monitors
the video feed. The system detects when each step begins and ends, validates the correct
sequence, and logs completion with timestamps. If a step is performed out of sequence or
skipped, the system displays alerts and prevents progression until corrected.

UC-003: Train System on Manufacturing Process
▪

A production supervisor records a reference video of an experienced operator performing
a new manufacturing process. The supervisor marks the start and end of each step, labels
them, and adds notes. The system saves the training session with metadata for use in
future process verification.

UC-004: Review Process Violation Log
▪

A production supervisor opens the violation log interface and filters by date, workstation,
or violation type. The supervisor selects individual violations to view detailed
information including timestamps, video footage, and operator IDs. After review, the
supervisor marks violations as reviewed and exports reports for management analysis.

UC-005: Simulate Workstation Environment
▪

A developer loads recorded camera footage into the simulation environment and injects
synthetic barcode data to test system functionality. The system processes the simulated
inputs, generates outputs, and displays verification results. The developer validates
system behavior before production deployment.

4.2.1.2. Data Requirements
The system depends on a combination of real-time camera data, barcode information, and
process-tracking records to support its monitoring and verification functions. Each data source
must be handled securely and consistently to ensure accuracy and reliability in a manufacturing
environment.
The main input data comes from live video streams captured by cameras at each workstation.
These frames are processed locally on edge devices to identify barcodes, track parts, and verify
worker actions. Each frame includes a timestamp and workstation ID to maintain
synchronization and traceability across multiple cameras.
All data is stored securely on the local network and managed according to the manufacturer’s
privacy and security policies. Sensitive information, such as worker identifiers or connection
credentials, is protected using encrypted .env files and role-based access control. These data
requirements ensure that the system maintains accurate and traceable records of all part and
process events, supporting efficient monitoring, auditing, and future scalability.

5. Exploratory Studies
5.1. Relevant Techniques
The exploratory phase focused on identifying computer-vision techniques that could operate
reliably in real manufacturing environments without constant retraining or cloud support.
Because each facility may use different layouts, materials, and workflows, the system needed to
be flexible, lightweight, and fully on-premises.
For part and material tracking, traditional vision methods proved the most effective. Barcode and
QR decoding libraries such as pyzbar [1] and ZXing [2] were evaluated as dependable solutions
for linking visual data to unique part identifiers. These techniques provide precise and verifiable
tracking of materials as they move through each stage of production, forming the backbone of
the system’s traceability layer.
For worker-step verification, a more adaptive and semantic approach was needed. The project
explored OpenCLIP [3] and MobileCLIP [4], which generate multimodal embeddings capable of
connecting visual input to textual task descriptions. By comparing live camera frames against
step definitions or reference images encoded in the same embedding space, the system can
determine whether the correct procedure is being followed without requiring model retraining for
each new process.
This hybrid strategy, symbolic tracking for materials, semantic understanding for actions, allows
the system to remain both accurate and flexible. Edge optimization and model quantization were
also studied to ensure that OpenCLIP [3] can operate efficiently on local GPU hardware,
maintaining real-time performance while keeping all data processing securely on-site.

5.2. Relevant Packages/Products
Several open-source frameworks and libraries were examined during the exploratory phase.
OpenCV [5] remained the foundation for video capture, preprocessing, and visualization.
OpenCLIP [3] was integrated through the PyTorch [6] ecosystem for embedding generation and
similarity matching. Though we cannot use the MobileCLIP [4] model itself as it falls under a
limit research license. FastAPI [7] was reviewed for real-time data exchange between the vision
modules, the LLM-based reasoning system, and the operator dashboard. Data management and
analysis relied on NumPy [8], Pandas [9], and SQL databases to record event logs and validation
results. Package management tools such as uv [10] and conda [11] were evaluated for
reproducible, isolated deployments suitable for regulated installations.

5.3. Broader Impacts
Using OpenCLIP [3] expands what’s possible for on-premises AI in manufacturing. These
models allow semantic understanding of human actions and part interactions without requiring
cloud-scale datasets or manual labeling, opening the door to more adaptive and intelligent
factory systems.

Team 9
In the long term, this technology can improve safety by recognizing incorrect procedures in real
time, support training by providing immediate visual feedback, and streamline audits by
automatically logging validated process steps. More broadly, it demonstrates how advanced AI
can be localized, offering powerful visual reasoning while preserving data ownership, privacy,
and compliance.

Git + GitHub: Source control and collaboration platform for version tracking,

VS Code: Primary IDE for development and debugging.

7.2. Coding Conventions
To maintain a consistent, readable, and maintainable codebase across the team, the project
follows standardized Python development practices. These conventions support collaboration,
ease of debugging, and future extensibility.
The development environment consists of Python 3.10+ on Ubuntu 22.04 LTS. We manage the
dependencies using the uv [10] library, which uses pyproject.toml and uv.lock to ensure
reproducible builds.
The use of inline comments to explain what the code does and why the decisions were made will
keep the code easy to read and continue further development on. Proper naming conventions will
be utilized in files, classes, methods, and variables to promote readability. Each subsystem will
include a short README.md to explain the use of said subsystem. Using Python's built-in
logging, we will be able to produce a verbose output that will let developers know exactly where
the code failed.

7.3. Code Version Control
All project code is managed using Git and hosted on GitHub, providing a centralized platform
for version tracking, collaboration, and peer review. The team follows a structured branching
workflow, with the main branch reserved for stable, production-ready releases and a dev branch
used for active development and integration testing. New features or fixes are developed in
dedicated feature branches (e.g., feature/<name> or fix/<name>), ensuring that changes remain
isolated until verified. Each commit will include a detailed message of what was changed. All
pull requests must be reviewed by at least one team member and successfully pass automated
testing before merging. Sensitive information, including API keys and credentials, is secured
through .env files and excluded from the repository via .gitignore. Access to the repository is
role-based, with maintainers responsible for approving merges into the main branch to preserve
stability and code integrity.

9. Challenges & Open Issues
9.1. Challenges Faced in Requirements Engineering
Defining clear requirements for this project has been an ongoing challenge due to its evolving
scope and layered communication structure. Unlike a traditional software project with fixed
specifications, this initiative is still in the developmental phase within the manufacturer’s broader
modernization effort. As a result, many system expectations continue to shift as the manufacturer
refines its own internal processes and priorities.
Another difficulty stems from the project’s two-in-one nature. AIBI Solutions is developing the
AI component while KMD Technology Solutions oversees the larger software integration. This
means that decisions are often split between technical design for AIBI’s on-premise AI system
and operational requirements defined by KMD’s project management tools. Balancing both sides
has required ongoing clarification to ensure that features remain aligned with the manufacturer’s
real-world workflow.
Communication flow also adds complexity. Because the development is being conducted for a
manufacturer through a software company, information moves through several layers before
reaching the engineering team. Questions and feedback can take time to travel back and forth,
which slows iteration and can cause temporary gaps in understanding.
Additionally, from the manufacturer’s perspective, the AI system is currently viewed as a lower
priority compared to its core production and infrastructure initiatives. This limits the amount of
dedicated feedback and testing time available, making it harder to gather detailed operational
requirements early on.
Many questions at this stage remain speculative, there are still unknowns about how the system
will integrate with existing machinery, what data streams will be available, and what the longterm performance expectations will look like. Because of that uncertainty, much of the early
requirements work has focused on flexibility and modularity rather than strict definitions.
Finally, testing in a production-like environment presents logistical challenges. Access to the
actual manufacturing floor is limited, and replicating realistic lighting, noise, and workflow
conditions off-site is difficult. This means that validating the system’s accuracy and reliability
under true operating conditions will likely occur later in the development cycle, increasing the
importance of careful simulation and staged deployment.
Overall, these challenges have required an adaptive, iterative approach to requirements
engineering, focusing on communication, flexibility, and traceability while allowing the
technical design to evolve alongside the manufacturer’s own readiness.

10. System Manuals
10.1. Instructions for System Development
System development for this project follows a structured process designed to maintain
consistency, reliability, and reproducibility across all team members and deployment
environments. All development is conducted using Python 3.10+ on Ubuntu Server 22.04 LTS,
ensuring compatibility with the on-premise infrastructure already in place at the manufacturer.
Dependency management is handled through uv [10], which provides deterministic builds which
enable developers to quickly synchronize environments across different workstations.
Development and debugging are performed using VS Code, chosen for its integration with Git,
Python, and FastAPI-based tooling.
All source code is tracked using Git and hosted on GitHub, following the branching workflow
established in Section 7.3. The main branch is reserved for stable, production-ready code, while
all active work occurs in the dev branch. All merges require successful test execution and peer
review. Commit messages follow a consistent format describing the scope of each change.
Sensitive credentials, including database connection strings and API keys, are never committed
to version control and are securely managed through .env files that are ignored by Git.
The system is organized into modular subsystems to ensure scalability and maintainability. The
barcode recognition subsystem uses OpenCV [5] for image preprocessing and Pyzbar [1] /
ZXing [2] for decoding one-dimensional and two-dimensional barcodes, while the worker-step
verification subsystem utilizes OpenCV and open_clip [3] to extract image embeddings and
verify human task completion through step-specific inference. Both subsystems rely on NumPy
[8] and Pandas [9] for numerical processing and structured data logging. Logging throughout the
system is handled using Python’s built-in logging library, providing detailed runtime information
to assist in debugging and error tracing. Each subsystem includes a README.md file describing
its purpose, structure, and key functions, ensuring that future developers can quickly understand
and extend the implementation.
All scan results, worker verification data, and configuration settings are stored in an on-premises
SQL Server database. Connections are established through environment-defined credentials and
utilize parameterized queries to maintain data integrity and security. Testing is an integral part of
the development workflow: unit tests are written with pytest while integration testing is
conducted using sample camera feeds and controlled production simulations. Benchmarking is
used to evaluate performance metrics such as frame rate, recognition accuracy, and latency under
realistic load conditions.
Deployment targets include both edge workstations and a central application server, each
running isolated uv environments for reproducibility. Logs and configuration files are maintained
in standardized directories on the host system. All changes to the system are documented through
inline code comments, updated README.md files, and version entries in CHANGELOG.md.
By adhering to these development instructions, the team ensures that the AIBI computer-vision
system remains modular, secure, and reproducible. The consistent use of Python, uv, and GitHub

provides a reliable foundation for collaboration, testing, and long-term maintainability, while the
project’s standardized structure supports ongoing refinement and expansion as new features and
subsystems are introduced.
